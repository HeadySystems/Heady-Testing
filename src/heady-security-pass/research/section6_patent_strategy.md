## 2. Alice/Mayo Section 101 Eligibility Strategies

### 2.1 The Two-Step Alice/Mayo Test (Current Framework)

The foundational eligibility test under 35 U.S.C. § 101 flows from *Alice Corp. v. CLS Bank International*, 573 U.S. 208 (2014) and *Mayo Collaborative Services v. Prometheus Labs., Inc.*, 566 U.S. 66 (2012):

```
Step 1: Is the claim directed to a process, machine, manufacture, or composition of matter?
         → If NO: ineligible
         → If YES: proceed to Step 2

Step 2A (Alice Step 1): Is the claim directed to a judicial exception?
   Prong 1: Does the claim RECITE an abstract idea (mathematical concept,
            mental process, or certain economic/business methods)?
   Prong 2: If so, does the claim as a whole INTEGRATE the exception into
            a practical application? (Look for: specific technical improvement,
            particular field of use, application to specific machine/transformation)

Step 2B (Alice Step 2): If directed to judicial exception WITHOUT practical
   application, does the claim add an "inventive concept" that amounts to
   "significantly more" than the abstract idea?
```

*Source: [USPTO MPEP § 2106](https://www.uspto.gov/web/offices/pac/mpep/s2106.html); [Thompson Patent Law Alice Analysis](https://thompsonpatentlaw.com/alice-software-patent-success/)*

### 2.2 Landmark Cases: 2024–2026

#### Recentive Analytics, Inc. v. Fox Corp. (Fed. Cir. Apr. 18, 2025)
**Citation**: 134 F.4th 1205 (Fed. Cir. 2025)
**Significance**: The Federal Circuit's **first major precedential decision addressing machine learning and Alice**.

- **Holdings**: Four patents (two "machine learning training" patents and two "network map" patents) directed to using ML to optimize TV broadcast schedules and generate network maps were **patent-ineligible**.
- **Critical language** from Judge Dyk: *"Whether claims that do no more than apply established methods of machine learning to a new data environment are patent eligible. We hold that they are not."*
- **What failed**: Claims merely applied conventional ML techniques to new data environments, without **any improvement to the ML technology or computer functioning itself**.
- **Analogy**: The court replaced "generic computer implementation" from prior abstract idea analysis with "generic machine learning implementation" — adding ML does not save claims unless it changes how the system operates.
- **Supreme Court**: Declined to grant certiorari in December 2025, leaving *Recentive* as controlling precedent.

*Source: [Dykema AI Patents 2025 Analysis](https://www.dykema.com/news-insights/ai-and-software-patents-in-2025-new-leadership-and-101-eligibility-guidance.html); [JD Supra Section 101 2025 Stories](https://www.jdsupra.com/legalnews/top-section-101-patent-eligibility-2852476/)*

#### Ex parte Desjardins (USPTO Appeals Review Panel, Sept. 26, 2025)
**Citation**: Appeal No. 2024-000567
**Significance**: USPTO directorial review decision under Director Squires — reversed § 101 rejection.

- **Holdings**: Improvements to the **functioning of machine learning models** can constitute practical applications under the subject matter eligibility framework.
- **Key shift**: Signaled willingness to treat ML model improvements as **technological improvements**, redirecting gatekeeper function toward §§ 102/103 rather than § 101.
- **Strategic implication**: If you can show your invention changes *how the model learns or operates* (not just *what it applies to*), § 101 rejection is beatable.

*Source: [Mayer Brown USPTO vs. Federal Circuit Analysis (Oct 2025)](https://www.mayerbrown.com/en/insights/publications/2025/10/uspto-encourages-ai-innovation-as-federal-circuit-exercises-caution)*

#### Ancora Technologies, Inc. v. Roku, Inc. (Fed. Cir. June 16, 2025)
**Citation**: No. 2023-1674
**Significance**: The Federal Circuit reaffirmed plain and ordinary meaning for claim terms like "agent" — will encompass both software and hardware implementations absent clear disclaimer.

- **Strategic implication**: Broad functional claim language (e.g., "agent") interpreted broadly; no need to artificially restrict to software-only or hardware-only when prosecution history is clean.

*Source: [Nixon Peabody analysis (Sept 2025)](https://www.nixonpeabody.com/insights/alerts/2025/09/02/federal-circuit-clarifies-aspects-of-ai-software-patent-standards)*

#### Pre-Alice Patents Continue to Fall (Feb. 2026)
Per [Patently-O (Feb 2026)](https://patentlyo.com/patent/2026/02/47735.html), three Federal Circuit § 101 decisions in one week invalidated pre-2010 filed patents. **Pre-Alice patent portfolios face persistent eligibility problems**. Newly drafted patents have significant advantage when built around current guidance.

### 2.4 Strategies for Overcoming § 101 Rejection

#### Strategy 1: "Recites vs. Involves" — Avoid Explicitly Naming Algorithms
The 2024 AI-SME Update draws a critical distinction:
- **RECITES a judicial exception**: "training a neural network using backpropagation and gradient descent" → triggers full § 101 analysis
- **INVOLVES a judicial exception**: "training a neural network using a training dataset" → merely involves mathematical concepts; may not require Step 2A/2B analysis

**Action**: Draft claims that describe functional outcomes without naming the specific mathematical algorithm in the claim language. Push algorithm specifics to the specification.

#### Strategy 2: Claim Concrete Technological Improvements (Not New Data Environments)
Based on *Recentive* and USPTO guidance, the dispositive question is: **does the claim improve the technology, or does it merely apply conventional technology to new data?**

**What fails**:
- "Apply machine learning to [new field]"
- "Train a model on [new type of data]"
- Wrapping existing AI in new domain language

**What succeeds** (per [Thompson Patent Law 2026](https://thompsonpatentlaw.com/artificial-intelligence-patents/)):
- Claims that reduce database query latency via specific architectural means
- Claims that improve model accuracy with less computing power through a specific technique
- Claims that enable new network functionality through a defined mechanism
- "The *how* of improvement" — specific technical disclosure of *how* results are achieved

#### Strategy 3: Anchor Claims to Specific Field + Specific Solution
Per the USPTO August 2025 Memo and [Caldwell Global (Aug 2025)](https://caldwelllaw.com/news/uspto-ai-patent-eligibility-2025-memo/), eligibility is aided by:
1. **Particular field of use** in claims (e.g., "in the field of network intrusion detection")
2. **Technical explanation of the improvement** in specification
3. **Non-abstract limiting terms** in claims reflecting the improvement (e.g., "dropping malicious packets in real-time after neural network identification" — not just "identifying threats")

#### Strategy 4: Use Subject Matter Eligibility Declarations (SMEDs)
As of December 2025, the USPTO formally recognizes **SMEDs** under 37 C.F.R. § 1.132 as admissible evidence to establish:
- Technological improvements that cannot practically be performed mentally
- State of the art at the time of filing
- Concrete technical benefits

**Best practice**: In close cases, prepare a SMED from a technical expert documenting the specific technical improvement and why the mental process grouping does not apply.

#### Strategy 5: Draft the Specification as a Technical Improvement Document
Based on USPTO guidance (especially Example 47), the specification should:
- Describe the **current state of the technology** and its limitations
- Articulate **what specific problem** the invention solves
- Explain the **specific mechanism** of improvement (not just "better performance")
- Include concrete measurements or benchmarks where possible

#### Strategy 6: System + Method Claim Ladder
Draft multiple independent claims:
1. **System claim** (apparatus) — harder to categorize as abstract
2. **Method claim** — broadest behavioral coverage
3. **Computer-readable medium claim** — covers software distribution
4. **Specific improvement claim** — directed to the narrow technical advance

Each claim level provides fallback positions during prosecution.

### 2.5 Patent Eligibility Restoration Act (PERA) 2025

**Status**: Reintroduced May 1, 2025 in both Senate and House with bipartisan sponsorship (Senators Tillis and Coons; Representatives Kiley and Peters).

**What it would do**: Eliminate the judicially-created "abstract idea," "law of nature," and "natural phenomenon" exceptions entirely. Replace with a narrower statutory framework — ineligible only if:
1. Mathematical formula *per se*
2. Process that is *substantially* economic/financial/business/social/cultural/artistic
3. Mental process occurring entirely in nature
4. Unmodified human gene as it exists in the body
5. Unmodified natural material as it exists in nature

**Critical provision**: "The claimed invention shall not be excluded from eligibility if the invention cannot practically be performed without the use of a machine or manufacture." — This would effectively restore software patent eligibility for AI inventions that require a computer.

**Strategic implication**: If PERA passes, many current § 101 strategies become unnecessary. File applications now with strong technical improvement framing (compliant with current law) but preserve specification breadth for potential PERA benefit.

*Source: [IPWatchdog PERA 2025 Analysis](https://ipwatchdog.com/2025/05/01/patent-eligibility-reform-returns-hill-pera-2025-explained/); [C4IP PERA Support](https://c4ip.org/why-c4ip-supports-the-patent-eligibility-restoration-act-of-2025-pera/)*

## 3. Continuation-in-Part (CIP) Strategy Framework

### 3.1 Legal Foundation

A **Continuation-in-Part (CIP)** is governed by 35 U.S.C. § 120 and MPEP § 201.08. It is the **only type of continuing application** that permits the introduction of new subject matter ("new matter") not disclosed in the parent application. The CIP:
- Contains a **substantial portion** of the parent's specification
- Adds **new technical material** (new embodiments, experiments, features)
- Creates **split priority dates**: old matter retains parent's priority date; new matter gets CIP filing date

*Source: [BlueIron IP — New Matter Priority](https://blueironip.com/ufaqs/how-does-new-matter-in-a-continuation-in-part-application-affect-priority-dates/); [DrugPatentWatch CIP Strategic Guide](https://www.drugpatentwatch.com/blog/the-double-edged-scalpel-a-strategic-guide-to-continuation-in-part-cip-applications-in-pharmaceutical-drug-development/)*

### 3.2 CIP vs. Continuation vs. New Application Decision Matrix

| Factor | Favors CIP | Favors Pure Continuation | Favors New Application |
|--------|------------|--------------------------|------------------------|
| New technical matter | Yes — specifically for CIP | No new matter permitted | Yes, full 20-yr term |
| Patent term impact | Loses years (20yr from parent) | Same as parent EBD | Full new 20-yr term |
| Prior art exposure | New matter exposed to intervening prior art | Same as parent | Same as new filing date |
| Portfolio consolidation | Strong — keeps family unified | Strong | Separates families |
| ODP risk | High (§ 121 safe harbor does NOT apply) | Moderate | Clean prosecution history |
| New matter is minor improvement | Ideal for CIP | Inappropriate | Overkill |
| New matter is major standalone invention | Risky (term loss) | Inappropriate | Preferred |
| Time elapsed since parent | <6 years from EBD (avoid new fees) | <6 years from EBD | Any time |

*Source: [PowerPatent CIP Priority Analysis](https://powerpatent.com/blog/how-priority-dates-work-in-continuations-and-cips); [Cooley GO Continuation Strategy](https://www.cooleygo.com/using-continuation-applications-strategically/)*

### 3.3 New USPTO Fee Structure for Continuing Applications (Effective Jan. 19, 2025)

The USPTO's November 2024 final rule created **significant new financial penalties for late-filed continuations, divisionals, and CIPs**:

| Time from Earliest Benefit Date (EBD) | Additional Fee (Large Entity) | Additional Fee (Small Entity) |
|----------------------------------------|-------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| 6 to <9 years after EBD | $2,700 (one-time surcharge) | $1,080 |
| 9+ years after EBD | $4,000 (one-time surcharge) | $1,600 |
| Under 6 years | $0 additional | $0 additional |

Additional context from [Calfee IP analysis (Jan 2025)](https://www.calfee.com/newsletters-738):
- Standard filing fees also increased ~7.5% across the board
- Independent claims over three: increased from $480 → $600 each
- Claims over 20: doubled to $200/claim
- Second and subsequent RCEs: increased 43% (to $2,860)
- IDS fees for >50 cited references: $200–$800 additional

**Strategic response**:
1. **File CIPs within 6 years of the parent EBD** to avoid the surcharge
2. For families with EBDs approaching the 5–6 year mark, audit now and file proactively
3. After 9 years from EBD, a second RCE ($2,860) costs ~$1,200 less than a new continuation ($4,000 surcharge + filing fee) — changes the RCE vs. continuation calculus significantly

*Source: [IPWatchdog Continuing Application Fee Rule](https://ipwatchdog.com/2025/01/08/new-uspto-fee-rule-continuing-applications-key-changes-strategic-considerations-applicants/)*

### 3.4 CIP Priority Date Mechanics

**The Split Priority Rule** (MPEP § 201.08):
- Claims relying **solely on shared disclosure** from parent → priority date = **parent application's filing date**
- Claims relying on **new matter** in the CIP → priority date = **CIP filing date**
- Claims using **both old and new matter** → effective date is the **CIP filing date** (the newer date)

**Patent term**: CIP patents expire 20 years from the **earliest** filed application in the priority chain (subject to patent term adjustment). A CIP filed in 2026 on a 2020 parent expires in **2040**, not 2046. This lost term can represent significant commercial value.

**Practical implication for AI platforms**: If your AI orchestration platform filed a provisional in 2023 and you add a new vector memory subsystem in 2026:
- Core orchestration claims → 2023 priority date
- Vector memory claims → 2026 priority date  
- Platform expires: 2043 (20 years from 2023 non-provisional)

### 3.5 Timing Best Practices for CIP Filings

1. **File before the parent issues** — Once a patent issues, you cannot file a continuation from it. File CIPs while the parent is still pending.

2. **File before the 6-year EBD mark** — Avoid the new $2,700–$4,000 surcharge.

3. **File when new matter is technically complete** — CIP must support full written description (35 U.S.C. § 112) for the new matter. File when you can describe the invention fully.

4. **Document new matter development timeline** — Create engineering records showing *when* new features were developed. Crucial for establishing which claims get which priority date.

5. **Consider strategic patent term** — If the new matter is a multi-billion dollar standalone invention (e.g., a breakthrough agentic architecture), filing a separate application preserves full 20-year term.

6. **Track PTAB double patenting risks** — CIPs do NOT receive § 121 safe harbor protection from obviousness-type double patenting (ODP). Terminal disclaimers may be required, linking CIP term to parent.

*Source: [LinkedIn CIP Basics (Jan 2026)](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=vl7tu-gqFaU); [DrugPatentWatch CIP Guide](https://www.drugpatentwatch.com/blog/the-double-edged-scalpel-a-strategic-guide-to-continuation-in-part-cip-applications-in-pharmaceutical-drug-development/)*

### 3.6 Recommended CIP Strategy for AI Platform Patent Families

For an AI platform with iterative development cycles, use a **rolling CIP architecture**:

```
Year 1: File PCT/provisional covering core platform architecture
Year 2: File non-provisional → enter PCT national phase
Year 2-3: File CIP1 → adds vector memory subsystem (within 6-yr window)
Year 3-4: File CIP2 from CIP1 → adds geometric routing engine
Year 4-5: File CIP3 from CIP2 → adds edge inference coordination
```

Each CIP must be filed while its parent is **pending** (not yet issued or abandoned). Keep at least one application in the family pending at all times. The chain must be unbroken.

### 4.2 Free Tools: Detailed Analysis

#### USPTO Patent Center (formerly EFS-Web/PAIR)
- **URL**: https://patentcenter.uspto.gov
- **Coverage**: All U.S. patent applications and grants; real-time prosecution status via Patent Center
- **Best use**: Verifying legal status, accessing prosecution history (file wrapper), checking application status
- **Limitation**: U.S.-only; no semantic search; interface requires patent search conventions
- **Critical capability**: Access to entire IDS citations submitted by applicants — valuable for understanding examiner-relevant prior art

#### Google Patents
- **URL**: https://patents.google.com
- **Coverage**: 18M+ indexed documents across major global offices; machine translation for foreign patents
- **Best use**: Quick prior art sweeps, citation network tracing, "prior art finder" for related documents
- **AI features**: Neural similarity search, semantic suggestions via "Similar patents" feature
- **Limitation**: Not suitable for formal patentability opinions or FTO reports; lacks analytics; not a substitute for professional search
- **Note**: Google Scholar integration in Lens.org enables cross-reference between patent and non-patent literature (NPL)

#### Espacenet (EPO)
- **URL**: https://worldwide.espacenet.com
- **Coverage**: 110M+ patents from 97 countries; strong EP and PCT coverage
- **Best use**: European patent prosecution; CPC classification-based searches; patent family analysis
- **Key feature**: "Browse by classification" using CPC/IPC codes — essential for AI-specific taxonomy (CPC class G06N covers ML, neural networks, AI)
- **Limitation**: Boolean query only; no semantic search; limited analytics

#### Lens.org
- **URL**: https://www.lens.org
- **Coverage**: 153.2M patents + 266M scholarly works; 417M citations
- **Best use**: Cross-referenced patent + academic prior art; NPL integration for AI patent work (academic papers often constitute prior art for AI claims)
- **Unique feature**: Completely open, API access, bulk data download — valuable for building custom AI prior art search pipelines
- **Limitation**: No advanced commercial analytics; weaker visualization tools

### 4.3 Professional/Enterprise Tools

#### PatSnap
- **Database**: 204M+ patents; domain-specific AI modules
- **AI capabilities**: Eureka AI agents decompose search queries into multi-step workflows; semantic similarity search reduces manual iteration by ~60%
- **Strengths**: Portfolio analytics, heat maps, inventor networks, competitive landscape visualization; integrates search and intelligence
- **Weaknesses**: Enterprise pricing (out of reach for solo practitioners); significant onboarding investment
- **URL**: https://www.patsnap.com

#### Derwent Innovation (Clarivate)
- **Database**: 165M+ publications; DWPI (Derwent World Patents Index) — human-curated, value-added abstracts
- **AI capabilities**: DWPI-trained semantic search; gold standard for chemistry and pharma
- **Strengths**: DWPI abstract quality for obscure technologies; deep citation analytics
- **Weaknesses**: Very high cost; complex interface; overkill for pure software AI searches

#### NLPatent (AI-Native)
- **Approach**: Proprietary LLMs trained specifically on patent language; accepts full invention disclosures in natural language
- **Reported performance**: 80% reduction in search time; finds conceptually similar patents missed by keyword approaches
- **Best use**: Patentability and invalidity searches where conceptual similarity matters
- **URL**: https://nlpatent.com

### 4.4 Recommended Workflow for AI Platform Prior Art Search

## 5. International Filing: PCT/EPO/WIPO Strategy

### 5.1 PCT System Overview

The **Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT)**, administered by WIPO, enables a single international application to preserve patent rights in 157+ member countries simultaneously. The PCT does not itself grant patents — it defers national examination while preserving priority.

**Key structural elements**:
- **Filing**: Through a Receiving Office (RO) — typically the USPTO for U.S. applicants
- **International Search Report (ISR)**: Conducted by an International Searching Authority (ISA) within ~16–18 months of priority date
- **International Preliminary Examination (IPE)**: Optional; provides preliminary patentability opinion
- **National Phase**: Entry into individual country/regional offices before the 30/31-month deadline

*Source: [WIPO PCT Time Limits](https://www.wipo.int/en/web/pct-system/texts/time_limits); [Teak IP PCT Guide](https://teakipservices.com/pct-national-phase-deadlines-complete-timeline-guide/)*

### 5.2 Critical Timeline: PCT Filing Sequence

```
DAY 0: File priority application (U.S. provisional or non-provisional)
        → This establishes your priority date
        
MONTH 12: DEADLINE — File PCT application
           → Preserves priority date across all PCT member states
           → Missing this date = loss of PCT benefits; can only file direct nationals
           
MONTH 16-18: International Search Report (ISR) issued
              → First look at prior art from international perspective
              → Opportunity to amend claims under Article 19
              
MONTH 22-23: Optional — Request International Preliminary Examination (IPE)
              → Request IPEA to re-examine after Article 34 amendments
              → Provides international preliminary report (Chapter II)
              
MONTH 28-29: STRATEGIC DEADLINE — Decide which countries to enter national phase
              → Prepare translations; engage local agents; pay national fees
              → Leave buffer; don't wait until 30/31 months
              
MONTH 30: National phase entry deadline for most countries
            → U.S.: 30 months from priority
            → EPO: 31 months from priority
            → Japan: 30 months
            → China: 30 months
            → India: 31 months
            → South Korea: 31 months
            → UK: 31 months
            → Canada: 30 months (with possible extension)
```

**CRITICAL**: Missing the national phase deadline in most countries results in **permanent loss of rights** in that jurisdiction. There are very limited reinstatement mechanisms. [Intepat IP (Feb 2026)](https://www.intepat.com/blog/late-pct-national-phase) notes that only certain jurisdictions offer "further processing" (EPO) or petition-based relief (UK, Canada), and these are subject to stringent due-care standards.

*Source: [WIPO PCT Time Limits Table](https://www.wipo.int/en/web/pct-system/texts/time_limits); [Teak IP PCT Deadline Guide](https://teakipservices.com/pct-national-phase-deadlines-complete-timeline-guide/)*

### 5.4 EPO Prosecution Strategy for AI/Software Patents

The EPO applies the **technical character requirement** under Article 52(2)–(3) EPC. Software and AI inventions must demonstrate a "technical character" — they must produce a "technical effect" that goes beyond the normal physical interactions between a program and the computer on which it runs.

**EPO 2025 Examination Framework** (from [J A Kemp EPO Software Decisions 2025](https://www.jakemp.com/knowledge-hub/review-of-epo-software-decisions-in-2025/)):

EPO Technical Boards of Appeal reviewed 222 software/AI decisions in 2025:
- **69% refused or revoked**
- 16% remitted to first instance
- 6% granted or maintained
- Non-technical cases: **83% rejection rate**

**What fails at the EPO (2025 case law)**:

| Case | Failure Reason |
|------|---------------|
| T 2082/22 (Deutsche Telekom) | Neural network classification per se is mathematical/non-technical |
| T 1309/23 | Sparse ML description ("laundry list" of models) insufficient |
| T 1717/22 (Climate LLC) | Management zones from crop data = economic consideration, not technical |
| T 1851/22 (Auctane) | Automation of pre-existing business steps = non-technical |
| T 0613/23 (Safran) | Model performance depends on non-technical factors (expert competence) |

**What succeeds at the EPO**:

| Case | Success Reason |
|------|---------------|
| T 1392/20 (Hilloa) | Secure app configuration on user device = system integration (technical) |
| T 2353/22 (AB Initio) | Data structures/pointers for memory access = technical even if goal is non-technical |
| T 2153/22 (CPLabs) | Smart contract execution on IoT = technical (remitted) |
| T 0799/24 (JFE Steel) | Simulation specifying "further technical use" (e.g., adding welds to improve stiffness) |

**EPO Claim Drafting Requirements**:
1. **Specify external/direct technical effects** — not mere model optimization or knowledge-gaining
2. **Simulations must specify "further technical use"** (per G1/19) — output must specify concrete technical application, not just design evaluation
3. **Provide full ML model detail** — topology, mathematical modeling of nodes, learning procedures (per T 1309/23); simply referencing "a neural network" or listing frameworks without implementation details fails sufficiency
4. **Explicit computer implementation** — claims must recite computer execution to avoid "mental act" exclusion
5. **Submit all claim amendments early** — RPBA (Rules of Procedure of the Boards of Appeal) restrict late claim amendments

**UK Supreme Court (Feb. 11, 2026)**: Issued landmark judgment on AI/software patentability adopting an "intermediate step" framework aligned with EPO approach — determining which features are "technical" vs. "non-technical" before assessing novelty and inventive step. [IPWatchdog analysis](https://ipwatchdog.com/2026/02/11/uk-supreme-court-issues-milestone-judgment-ai-software-patentability/).

## 6. AI-Specific Patent Claim Drafting Strategies

### 6.1 Foundational Principles for AI Claim Drafting

Based on synthesis of 2025–2026 USPTO guidance, Federal Circuit decisions, and EPO case law, the following principles govern successful AI patent claims:

**The "How" Imperative**: Per [Thompson Patent Law analysis (2026)](https://thompsonpatentlaw.com/artificial-intelligence-patents/) citing *Recentive*, claims must disclose the "how" of the improvement. It is not sufficient to claim:
- "Using machine learning to optimize X"
- "Training a model on X data"
- "Applying a neural network to solve Y"

Claims must specify **the specific mechanism, architecture, or novel training procedure** by which the improvement is achieved.

**Avoid Generic AI Wrappers**: Per [PatSnap cross-jurisdiction guide (Dec 2025)](https://www.patsnap.com/resources/blog/articles/ai-patent-claims-cross-jurisdiction-guide-2025/), successful 2025 AI patent applications avoid generic terms like "neural network" or "machine learning" in favor of specific technical implementations. This approach improves patentability assessment by up to 40% based on grant data.

**System vs. Method Claim Strategy**: Both claim types are needed:
- **System claims** (apparatus): Harder to categorize as pure abstract ideas; often pass § 101 more readily
- **Method claims**: Broader coverage; essential for process-based innovations
- **CRM claims**: Cover software-in-use scenarios

### 6.2 AI Orchestration System Claims

**Technology description**: Systems where a "conductor" AI coordinates multiple specialized AI agents or models to accomplish complex, multi-step tasks.

**Target claim elements for eligibility**:
1. **Defined agent role architecture** — Not just "multiple agents" but specific coordination mechanism
2. **Transition criteria logic** — How the orchestrator decides which agent handles which subtask
3. **State management** — How the system maintains context across agent handoffs
4. **Tool-use interface** — Specific API or protocol for agent-tool interaction
5. **Error/fallback handling** — Specific recovery architecture

**Reference patents**:
- US 12,379,948 — Covers agentic AI integration with defined roles and coordination mechanisms ([JD Supra](https://www.jdsupra.com/legalnews/navigating-the-new-landscape-of-ai-9188116/))
- US 12,353,827 — Improvement of LLMs via non-LLM systems
- US 11,886,800 — Specific innovations in transformer model architecture

**Sample independent system claim structure** (illustrative framework):
```
A computing system for coordinating AI agents, comprising:
  - an orchestration processor configured to:
    - receive a task description
    - [specific mechanism] determine which agent class should handle [specific subtask type]
      based on [specific criteria — e.g., embedding similarity score against agent capability 
      profile, exceeding threshold T]
    - transmit [specific structured data format] to the selected agent
    - receive agent output comprising [specific data structure]
    - [specific aggregation/synthesis mechanism] synthesize outputs from multiple agents
      when [specific condition, e.g., confidence score below threshold]
  - a memory subsystem storing:
    - agent capability profiles comprising [specific fields]
    - task state representations in [specific format]
  wherein the orchestration processor reduces [specific measurable technical parameter,
  e.g., latency, token usage, error rate] compared to [specific baseline] by [specific 
  mechanism].
```

**What to avoid**:
- "An AI orchestration system comprising a controller that uses machine learning to manage agents" → too generic; fails *Recentive*
- Claim that reads on a single-agent system with trivial renaming

**White space**: Specific protocols for inter-agent communication, conflict resolution between agent recommendations, and dynamic agent spawning/termination based on task complexity.

### 6.3 Vector Memory System Claims

**Technology description**: Systems that store, index, and retrieve information using high-dimensional vector embeddings for semantic similarity search.

**Dense prior art (avoid or design around)**:
- US20240168978A1 — Scalable vector database for multi-tenant environments (nearest neighbor search)
- US20210365424A1 — Vector data storage with storage space reduction

**Available white space**:
1. **Orchestration-level vector routing** — Using embedding similarity not just for data retrieval but for agent selection and task routing
2. **Tiered memory architectures** — Combining vector memory with episodic, semantic, and working memory in a specific architecture
3. **Dynamic embedding optimization** — Methods that update embeddings in real-time based on usage patterns without full reindexing
4. **Privacy-preserving vector search** — Encrypted vector operations, federated similarity search

**Claim strategy**:
- Emphasize **specific indexing mechanisms** that improve performance characteristics (latency, accuracy trade-off) over prior art HNSW/IVF approaches
- Claim **integration architecture** between vector memory and the orchestration layer
- If claiming retrieval-augmented generation (RAG): focus on **specific prompt structuring techniques**, **context window management algorithms**, or **relevance scoring methods** that are novel architectural decisions

**EPO concern**: Pure vector similarity computation is mathematical. For EPO, tie claims to specific hardware memory management improvements, reduced cache misses, or specific computational efficiency with measurable external effect.

### 6.4 Geometric Routing Claims

**Technology description**: Using geometric principles (distance metrics, hyperbolic spaces, graph topology) to route queries, tasks, or inference requests to appropriate AI models or compute nodes.

**Existing prior art**:
- US20210297324A1 (GNN table-less routers) — GNN-based IP routing eliminating routing table lookup
- US20200279151A1 (DeepMind/GDM graph neural networks) — Structured representations and routing paths

**Patentable differentiators**:
1. **AI-specific geometric routing**: Routing AI inference requests using feature space geometry (not IP addresses)
2. **Hyperbolic space routing**: Using hyperbolic geometry for hierarchical AI task routing
3. **Latency-aware geometric selection**: Combining semantic similarity with latency/resource constraints in routing decision
4. **Dynamic topology updating**: Real-time updating of routing topology based on model performance metrics

**Claim approach**:
```
A method of routing inference requests to AI models, comprising:
  - generating a task embedding by [specific mechanism] from a received query
  - computing, using [specific distance metric in specific geometric space], a 
    proximity score between the task embedding and each of a plurality of 
    model capability profiles stored in [specific data structure]
  - selecting an AI model based on [specific combination of proximity score 
    and resource utilization metric]
  - [specific routing/dispatch mechanism] transmitting the query to the 
    selected model
  wherein the routing [specifically reduces latency / improves utilization / 
  reduces error rate] by [specific measurable amount] compared to [specific 
  prior art baseline].
```

**Technical improvement hook**: The key § 101 argument is that the geometric routing improves **computer system efficiency** (reduces unnecessary computation, minimizes model cold-starts, balances load) — this is a tangible technical improvement to computer functioning, not an abstract business optimization.

### 6.5 Multi-Agent Coordination Claims

**Technical description**: Protocols enabling multiple autonomous AI agents to collaborate, share state, negotiate task allocation, and avoid conflicting actions.

**Current patent landscape**: Per [Samuel Solomon IP LinkedIn (Feb 2026)](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/samuel-solomon-phd-rac-3324878_101-agentic-ai-patent-landscape-agentic-activity-7432630972111609856-gRDx), the landscape has shifted from protecting training methods to **system architecture and multi-agent orchestration** — still in early stages, presenting filing opportunity.

**Key distinguishable technical features** (per [JD Supra agentic AI](https://www.jdsupra.com/legalnews/navigating-the-new-landscape-of-ai-9188116/)):
1. **Agent role definition schema** — Specific data structure defining agent capabilities and permission boundaries
2. **Transition triggers** — Specific criteria (e.g., confidence threshold, task type classification, latency constraint) for handing off between agents
3. **Conflict resolution protocol** — When two agents produce contradictory outputs
4. **Shared memory management** — How agents read/write to shared state without race conditions
5. **Audit trail architecture** — Specific mechanism for tracing which agent made which decision

**Successful patent examples**:
- US 12,379,948 — Integration of specific AI agents with "defined roles and coordination mechanisms"
- Focus: architecture, coordination mechanisms, role definitions — NOT just "multiple agents working together"

**§ 101 strategy**: Frame as improving **system reliability and correctness** of multi-agent AI systems — the specific coordination protocol prevents conflicts that would cause incorrect outputs (concrete technical improvement to system functioning).

**EPO strategy**: Emphasize that the coordination protocol solves a **technical problem in distributed computing systems** (concurrency, consistency, fault tolerance) rather than a business coordination problem.

### 6.6 Edge AI Inference Claims

**Technology description**: Methods and systems for running AI model inference on resource-constrained edge devices (IoT, mobile, automotive) with limited compute, memory, and power.

**Claim strategies**:

1. **Model compression claims**: Specific quantization, pruning, or knowledge distillation methods that achieve measurable performance-accuracy trade-off improvements on specific hardware classes

2. **Federated inference claims**: Methods for distributing inference computation across multiple edge devices and aggregating results; specific protocols for when to offload to cloud vs. process locally

3. **Adaptive inference claims**: Dynamic selection of model complexity based on real-time resource state (battery level, network latency, memory availability); specific decision algorithms

4. **Hardware-aware optimization claims**: Methods for generating model architectures optimized for specific instruction set architectures (ARM, RISC-V) or specific accelerator types (NPU, DSP)

**Key § 101 argument**: Edge AI claims inherently involve **hardware optimization** (memory management, power consumption, compute cycles) — these are classic technical improvements to computing systems. Tie claims to specific hardware metrics:
- "reduces memory footprint by X% on [specific hardware class]"
- "reduces inference latency from X ms to Y ms under [specific conditions]"
- "maintains accuracy above Z% while operating within [specific power budget]"

**EPO consideration**: Edge AI with specific hardware constraints has a **clear technical character** — resource management on constrained hardware is unambiguously technical. This is a stronger EPO filing candidate than pure algorithm patents.

### 6.7 Functional Claiming and Means-Plus-Function (§ 112(f)) Strategy

#### Understanding § 112(f)
Per [IPWatchdog analysis (Sept 2024)](https://ipwatchdog.com/2024/09/15/role-functional-language-ai-patent-claims/) and [Finnegan analysis](https://www.finnegan.com/en/insights/articles/means-plus-function-claiming-what-does-it-mean-to-be-a-means.html), § 112(f) is triggered when:

(A) The claim uses "means" or "step" or a **nonce term** (generic placeholder: "module," "engine," "unit," "component," "system") for performing a function
(B) Modified by functional language linked by "for" or "configured to"
(C) NOT modified by sufficient structure

**Three-prong test for § 112(f) applicability** (MPEP § 2181):
- Even without the word "means," terms like "processing module," "detection unit," or "optimization engine" may invoke § 112(f) if deemed structurally empty
- When § 112(f) applies, claim scope is **limited to the specific algorithms in the specification** — narrow but potentially indefinite

#### Strategies to Avoid Problematic § 112(f) Interpretation

**Strategy A: Structural claiming** (preferred for system claims)
- Use specific structural terms: "transformer encoder," "attention mechanism," "key-value cache," "embedding layer" — these have recognized structure in the AI field
- Avoid: "processing means," "optimization module" alone
- Better: "transformer-based language processing unit comprising [specific layers and connections]"

**Strategy B: Algorithm disclosure in specification** (hedge for functional claims)
- Even if § 112(f) is not intended, ensure the specification discloses the specific algorithm for every functional limitation
- "Black box" descriptions without algorithmic detail create indefiniteness risk (*Ex Parte Joon Woo Son*)

**Strategy C: Multiple claim types — functional and structural**
- Draft broad method claims with functional language (broader scope)
- Draft narrow system claims with structural language (more defensible)
- Use dependent claims to add algorithmic specificity as fallback positions

**Note from 2024 Ancora v. Roku (Fed. Cir.)**: Terms like "agent" will receive their plain and ordinary meaning (both software and hardware) absent explicit disclaimer. This is **favorable for AI claims** — broad functional terms covering multiple implementation modalities are acceptable without automatically triggering § 112(f).

### 6.8 Recommended Claim Portfolio Architecture for AI Platform

```
INDEPENDENT CLAIM SET (per application):

Claim 1: SYSTEM (apparatus)
  - Named structural components with specific interconnections
  - Key technical feature stated in hardware/software terms
  - Specific technical improvement stated as objective result

Claim 2: METHOD (process)
  - Steps corresponding to system claim
  - Functional language anchored to specific data transformations
  - "Wherein" clause articulating technical improvement

Claim 3: CRM (computer-readable medium)
  - Instructions for method steps
  - Covers distribution scenario

DEPENDENT CLAIMS (priority order):
Claims 4-8: Narrow the core technical innovation
  - Specific architecture (e.g., number of agents, memory type)
  - Specific algorithm used in key step
  - Specific performance parameters

Claims 9-14: Specific embodiments
  - Different hardware configurations
  - Different agent types or model types
  - Different deployment scenarios (cloud, edge, hybrid)

Claims 15-20: Combination claims
  - Combinations with complementary innovations
  - Specific integration points with other system components
```

**Cost consideration**: Under 2025 fee rules, independent claims over 3 cost $600 each; claims over 20 cost $200 each. For a 20-claim application with 3 independent claims: standard fees apply. For 25 claims: add $1,000 in excess claims fees.

## 7. Strategic Synthesis & Recommendations

### 7.1 The 2025–2026 Patent Strategy Landscape in Brief

The AI patent landscape in 2025–2026 is characterized by three simultaneous forces:

1. **Federal Circuit tightening** (*Recentive*): Generic AI application to new domains is insufficient — claims must show architectural or methodological improvements to technology itself

2. **USPTO loosening** (Aug 2025 memo, *Desjardins*): Examiners directed to find eligibility where claims show specific technical improvements; "close call" rejections discouraged; ML model improvements can qualify

3. **Congressional potential reset** (PERA 2025): If passed, would largely eliminate § 101 as a barrier for AI patents, shifting all battles to §§ 102/103

**Strategic implication**: File now with strong technical improvement framing (satisfies current law), preserve specification breadth (benefits from PERA if passed), and invest in § 102/103 distinguishing in prosecution.
